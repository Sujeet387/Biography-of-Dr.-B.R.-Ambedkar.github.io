Dr. B.R. Ambedkar

1891-1956

Symbol of Knowledge

Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar (14 April 1891 ‚Äì 6 December 1956) was an Indian jurist, economist, social reformer and political leader who headed the committee drafting the Constitution of India from the Constituent Assembly debates, served as Law and Justice minister in the first cabinet of Jawaharlal Nehru, and inspired the Dalit Buddhist movement after renouncing Hinduism. Ambedkar graduated from Elphinstone College, University of Bombay, and studied economics at Columbia University and the London School of Economics, receiving doctorates in 1927 and 1923 respectively and was among a handful of Indian students to have done so at either institution in the 1920s. He also trained in the law at Gray's Inn, London. In his early career, he was an economist, professor, and lawyer. His later life was marked by his political activities; he became involved in campaigning and negotiations for India's independence, publishing journals, advocating political rights and social freedom for Dalits, and contributing significantly to the establishment of the state of India. In 1956, he converted to Buddhism, initiating mass conversions of Dalits. In 1990, the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award, was posthumously conferred on Ambedkar.   • B.R. Ambedkar was born on April 14, 1891, in the town of Mhow in Madhya Pradesh, India. He was the 14th and last child of his parents. His father was a Subedar in the Indian Army, and his mother was a housewife. Ambedkar faced social discrimination due to his caste from a young age, which led to his interest in social reform.

Fight against Caste Discrimination:

  • Ambedkar dedicated his life to fighting against caste discrimination and untouchability. He founded the Bahishkrit Hitakarini Sabha in 1924 to work for the welfare of the untouchables. He also founded the Independent Labour Party in 1936 to provide political representation to the oppressed communities.

Role in Drafting the Indian Constitution:

  • Ambedkar played a significant role in drafting India's Constitution, which came into effect on January 26, 1950. He served as the Chairman of the Drafting Committee of the Constituent Assembly and was instrumental in the inclusion of provisions for social justice and equality.

Political Career:

  • Ambedkar was a member of the Rajya Sabha from 1952 until his death in 1956. He served as the Minister of Law and Justice in the Nehru government from 1947 to 1951. He also founded the Republican Party of India in 1956 to promote the rights of the oppressed communities.

Contribution to Economics:

  • Ambedkar was a trained economist and made significant contributions to the field. He published several research papers and books, including "The Evolution of Provincial Finance in British India," "The Problem of the Rupee: Its Origin and Its Solution," and "Who Were the Shudras?" He advocated for economic and social reforms to uplift the oppressed communities.

Conversion to Buddhism:

  • n 1956, Ambedkar converted to Buddhism, along with thousands of his followers, to escape the caste system's discrimination. He believed that Buddhism provided a way to achieve spiritual and social equality.

Legacy:

  • B.R. Ambedkar's contributions to social reform, constitutional law, and economics have had a lasting impact on India. He is widely regarded as the father of the Indian Constitution and the champion of social justice. His ideas and teachings continue to inspire social and political movements in India and around the world. His birth anniversary, April 14, is celebrated as Ambedkar Jayanti in India.

Key Contributions:

Ambedkar made several significant contributions during his lifetime, including:

  • Drafting India's Constitution: Ambedkar played a pivotal role in drafting India's Constitution, which is considered one of the most extensive and progressive constitutions in the world.
  • Campaigning Against Caste Discrimination: Ambedkar spent most of his life fighting against caste discrimination, untouchability, and social inequality. He believed that social reform was essential to achieving political and economic freedom.
  • Education and Empowerment: Ambedkar emphasized the importance of education as a tool for empowering the oppressed communities. He founded several educational institutions, including the People's Education Society and the Siddharth College of Arts and Science.
  • Women's Rights: Ambedkar was a strong advocate for women's rights and believed that gender equality was essential for social progress. He played a key role in the Hindu Code Bill, which aimed to reform Hindu family law and provide equal rights to women.

Awards and Recognition:

  • Ambedkar received several awards and honours during his lifetime, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award, in 1990, nearly four decades after his death. He was also posthumously awarded the Columbia University Medal for Excellence in 2016, recognizing his contributions to the fields of law, economics, and social reform.

Death:

  • Ambedkar passed away on December 6, 1956, at the age of 65, due to complications from diabetes. His death was a significant loss for the social reform movement in India, but his legacy continues to inspire generations of activists and scholars.
